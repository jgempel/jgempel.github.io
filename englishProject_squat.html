How to Perform a Squat



Introduction:


The squat is the third and final exercise out of the three most basic powerlifting lifts. It is very similar to a deadlift because this exercise involves bending one’s hips, knees, and legs with an item in hand. In other words, the squats require a person to stand up, lower his or her hips close to the ground, and stand back up again.[26]. However, the deadlift requires the weight or item to be positioned below the stomach. The squat involves positioning the item above the stomach, typically behind one’s upper back[27]. Like the deadlift, this exercise must be completed while keeping one’s entire back straight.

To put it simply, performing a squat is very similar to sitting down in an invisible chair. Squats focus almost exclusively on strengthening the lower body. This exercise’s main focus is on the glutes (or butt muscles), adductors (or the thigh muscles near the groin area), quadriceps (or front thigh muscles), and lower back muscles . Although not as effective in this area, squats can also strengthen the calves (or rear lower leg muscles)[28].


            Source: StrengthLog[29]


Materials:


The following materials required for the bench press are labelled and described below. The numbers on the diagram correspond to each material needed, except for Number 5.

<             Source: Mira Fit[30]


DISCLAIMER: The above diagram illustrates a squat rack with a bench, which is not required for this exercise. Some squat racks may include a bench that can assist with squats and safe lifting practices. They can also be used for exercises not mentioned on this website.


  1. A barbell—a long, metallic rod that must be lifted by the exerciser.
  2. A squat rack—used to position the barbell when finished performing the squat. It should include a barbell holder to secure the barbell when not in use.

    3. WARNING: Please make sure your squat rack comes with a barbell holder. Failure to do so will prevent the lifter from safely setting down the barbell after an exercise. Throat, chest, or head injuries can occur due to the barbell potentially crushing those body parts.

  3. (Optional) Set of circular weights evenly distributed on the barbell.
  4. Barbell clips to secure the circular weights. Refer to the Home Page's "Getting Started" section for instructions.

    5. WARNING: Failure to use barbell clips can cause foot or other bodily injuries due to the circular weights falling off the barbell.

  5. A spotter—a person who stands beside you and ensures that you are safely performing the bench press. Highly recommended for beginning lifters.

DISCLAIMER: This web page only demonstrates a type of squat specifically performed with a squat rack and barbell. Variations of this exercise will not be covered here.


Step 1: Locate and Setup your Squat Rack


    NOTE: Throughout Step 1, make sure the barbell is off the squat rack and no weights are attached to it.


    DISCLAIMER: Setup processes can vary among different squat racks. This website does not provide detailed instructions on setting up a specific squat rack’s height. If you are unsure on how to complete Step 1, ask a gym employee or spotter for assistance.


  1. Find a squat rack in your gym. As mentioned above, make sure it has a barbell holder.
    • WARNING: If the squat rack does not have a barbell holder, DO NOT USE IT. Ensuring that a barbell holder comes equipped with the squat rack maximizes the safety of yourself and others.
  2. Set the proper height of the barbell from the squat rack. “Proper height” means that the barbell is positioned at the same height as your collarbone or shoulders[31].
    • If the height is too low, then this exercise will require you to bend your back. Your back should never be bent during this exercise[31].
      • WARNING: This situation reduces the strength you have to perform the squat, which can cause a serious injury.
    • If the height is too high, then the barbell will be very difficult to reach. In other words, you will be forced to stand on your toes[31].
      • WARNING: This situation reduces the strength you have to perform the squat, which can cause a serious injury.

Step 2: Test the Height of the Barbell on the Squat Rack


    NOTE: Throughout Step 2, make sure that no weights are attached to the barbell. This instruction will make it easier to adjust the squat rack’s height.


  1. Place the barbell on the squat rack’s barbell holder
    • A spotter is recommended for this step if you are an absolute beginner. If you have a spotter, lift one end of the barbell. Ask the spotter to lift the other end of the barbell. Carry the barbell to the workout area with the spotter’s help.
  2. Stand next to the barbell with your upper body touching it.
  3. Without lifting the barbell, place your hands on the barbell with an overhand grip, about shoulder width apart.
  4. Determine whether the barbell is:
    • Slightly above your upper chest.
    • Slightly below your shoulder.
  5. If Sub-Step 4 is satisfied, then the squat rack’s height is properly set. If not, then readjust the height until Sub-Step 4 is satisfied.

    6.             Source: Hygieia Strength and Conditioning[32]


  6. Insert any circular weights on the barbell if necessary. Make sure you use a barbell clip.
    • NOTE: More detailed instructions about installing circular weights on a barbell can be found on the Home Page under the "Getting Started" section.

Step 3: Position your Body in the Proper Form


WARNING: You need to be able to stand back two or three steps from the squat rack to perform the exercise. Make sure you have enough room to do so. If you are in a gym, make sure that other people are out of your way before proceeding.

  1. Stand behind the barbell as close to the middle of the squat rack as possible.
  2. Position your feet shoulder-width apart. Point your toes slightly away from your body.

    3.              Source: Built with Science[33]


  3. Place your hands on the barbell with an overhand grip. Make sure:
    1. Your hands are about 6-12 inches away from your shoulders.
    2. Your forearms are parallel to your torso.
  4. Rest the barbell on your upper back, just below your neck.

    5.             Source: BarBend.com[34]


Step 4: Perform the Lift


    WARNING: You need to be able to stand back two or three steps from the squat rack to perform the exercise. Make sure you have enough room to do so. If you are in a gym, make sure that other people are out of your way before proceeding.

  1. Maintain your form, as explained in Step 3. Keep your back straight, then push your chest muscles outward.
  2. While in your current position, use your legs and shoulders to lift the barbell off the squat rack’s barbell holder.
  3. Step backwards a few steps away from the squat rack.
    • NOTE: A spotter is recommended for this step. If you have a spotter, make sure that he or she tells you whether anybody is behind you. This step will prevent other individuals nearby from making contact with the barbell.
  4. Breathe inward slowly, then use your leg muscles to slowly descend your body. The position should appear as if you are sitting in a chair.

    5.             Source: Everkinetic[35]


  5. Keep descending your body until your thighs are practically parallel to the ground.
    • NOTE: During this sub-step, you may notice that your eyes are staring slightly at the ground. This situation indicates that you are maintaining a proper form.
  6. To lift your body upwards, breathe outward, then use your leg muscles to rise upward to a standing position.

    7.             Source: Everkinetic[36]


  7. Repeat Steps 4.4 through 4.6 four more times, or as recommended by a spotter or trainer.
  8. When you are finished with the exercise, step forward towards the squat rack and place the barbell in the barbell holder. Make sure you maintain a proper standing position for this step.
